The Children's Act 38 of 2005

Your definitive guide to parental rights, responsibilities, and the well-being of your child.

Module 1: Introduction to the Children's Act

While the Constitution is the supreme law, the Children's Act 38 of 2005 is the most important piece of national legislation dealing specifically with children. It gives effect to the constitutional rights of children. For fathers, understanding this Act is non-negotiable. It defines your rights, your responsibilities, and the legal framework for your relationship with your child.

A. Purpose of the Act (Section 2)

The Act's main goals are to protect children from harm, promote their well-being, and give effect to their constitutional rights. Crucially, it sets out principles to guide all decisions concerning children, always emphasizing that the child's best interests are paramount. This Act moves away from the old concept of parents having "custody" or "ownership" over a child, and instead talks about Parental Responsibilities and Rights (PR&R).

The Bundle of Parental Responsibilities & Rights

B. Who has Parental Responsibilities and Rights?

This is a critical concept. The Act automatically gives full PR&R to:

  • The child's biological mother.
  • The child's biological father, IF he is married to the mother, or was married to her at the time of conception or birth.

But importantly for unmarried fathers, Section 21 of the Act grants you full PR&R if you meet certain conditions, such as living with the mother in a permanent life-partnership or consenting to be identified as the father and contributing to the child's upbringing and maintenance. This was a major step forward for fathers' rights in South Africa.
